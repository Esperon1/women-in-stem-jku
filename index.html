Mina Saidze

Tech Executive & Author

Mina Saidze is a Data Scientist, best-selling author of 'Fair Tech', and founder of Inclusive Tech. Recognized as a Forbes 30 Under 30 and LinkedIn Top Voice, she is one of Germany's leading AI thought leaders.

Her Story & Blueprint

As the daughter of political activists from Afghanistan, she learned early on what it means to fight for equality and social justice.

Entering the AI and Big Data sectors from a non-traditional background, she proved that fresh perspectives are one's strongest asset. This drive led her to found Europe's first initiative for diversity in tech and AI ethics. Today, she is widely recognized as one of Germany's leading AI thought leaders.

For young women entering STEM, she offers a highly practical blueprint for success: Own Your Uniqueness, Trade Fear for Curiosity, Build Your Alliance, Treat Coding Like a Language, and Find Your "Why".

Prof. Dame Athene Donald

Experimental Physics

Professor Dame Athene Donald is a retired Professor of Experimental Physics at the University of Cambridge. She served as the University's Gender Equality Champion and authored a book on the topic.

A message from Prof. Donald

My name is Professor Dame Athene Donald and I am retired now but was for many years a Professor of Experimental Physics at the University of Cambridge.

I fell in love with Physics as soon as I was introduced to it at high school, although there were no scientists in the family. It was an all girls' school and it simply didn't occur to me that it was an odd subject for a girl to do. It was only when I went to University I realised how few women there were on my course.

I've never doubted that Physics was what I wanted to do. When I noticed how few women there were around me I just felt I had to get on with it. This was, of course, many years ago now so perhaps it felt less surprising as there were relatively few women in any of the professions at the time. It didn't cause me to feel I should give up though.

However, later in my career, when I was already a professor, I found far more difficulties, I suspect around stereotyping and general hostility that I had broken into a man's world. At that point I had quite consciously to think I will not let these people drive me out. Whether if I had been at another University than Cambridge I would have felt the same it's hard to know. It was at this time I started to work around gender equality and in due course became the University's Gender Equality Champion. In due course I wrote a book on the topic.

Of course. There is always going to be the question of when is the 'right' time to have a family. I was lucky in that my husband (also an academic) was willing to let my career flourish by giving his up. That was really unusual at the time, although I think more families now consider who shoudl be the primary carer before they start a family. Even so, I often felt guilty that I wasn't more around for the children.

If STEM is your dream career, stick with it. Yes there will be challenges and only you can decide if your love of science is greater than the challenges you may face. But that is true in any sector. The important thing is to follow your dream, and to find mentors and supporters to help you on your way.

Prof. Hiromi Yokoyama

Science & Society

Prof. Hiromi Yokoyama is a professor at the University of Tokyo. She specializes in science and technology studies, focusing on AI ethics, distributive justice, and diversity in STEM fields.

A message from Prof. Yokoyama

I am a professor at the University of Tokyo, specializing in the relationship between science and society. My areas of expertise are science and technology studies and the sociology of science. Recently, I have been interested in the issues surrounding AI and society, particularly those related to ethics and justice. The fact that AI is being used in the military is a given, but the rapid construction of data centers and the resulting burden on residents are problematic from the perspective of distributive justice. I am also concerned about the widening gap in inequality caused by AI. Furthermore, how AI will transform scientific research and how the role of human researchers will change are important topics. I have also been conducting research on topics such as trust in scientists and the reasons for the low number of women in STEM fields.

I became interested in physics when I was in junior high school. The reason was simple: I was educated in a Catholic school, so I was curious about how God created the universe. A friend's mother (who I later learned was a researcher) recommended a science magazine to me, and when I read it, I was shocked to find a discussion of inflation theory! I became very fond of physics, and in one year, when I was 15, I read 70 books on popular cosmology. I then compiled parts of them into reports.

I also enjoyed writing, so from that time on, I wanted to become a science journalist. However, in high school, my greatest interest was in neutrinos, a type of elementary particle. Japan was strong in this field, and it was a hot area, later producing two Nobel laureates in physics (Masatoshi Koshiba and Takaaki Kajita). Therefore, I decided I wanted to study physics first. I was fortunate to participate in a neutrino research team and work with Nobel laureates. I learned what science truly is.

The percentage of female students in STEM fields in Japan was extremely low, and especially low in physics. But I didn't mind. I was overjoyed to be able to study physics. I had no anxieties about the future. I thought things would work out somehow. However, my parents and relatives were concerned and opposed my decision to major in physics. Nevertheless, they were won over by my enthusiasm and supported me until I earned my doctorate.

In my younger years, I struggled with microaggressions. I often felt that my important opinions were not being taken seriously. Furthermore, male researchers were reluctant to invite female researchers to collaborate, leading to feelings of isolation within the community. I was able to overcome these problems by taking initiative and collaborating with many male researchers.

I love my job, so I tend to get absorbed in it. However, I've become accustomed to maintaining a work-life balance while prioritizing what's most important in my life. Fortunately, I'm naturally the type of person who can handle a lot of work. Maintaining balance leads to well-being, and as a result, I can achieve satisfying results in all aspects of my life.

No one knows what the future holds. However, your future is created by what you do now. Cherish the present, and work hard at what you love and what you want to do. The path will naturally open up for you, so I don't think you need to worry about the future.

Prof. Elisa Donati

Neuromorphic AI

Prof. Elisa Donati holds a PhD in Robotics and is an Adjunct Professor at the Institute of Neuroinformatics, UZH/ETH. She specializes in neuromorphic engineering and biomedical AI research.

A message from Prof. Donati

My background in STEM has been quite varied, and in many ways I have always tried to find my own path. I studied Biomedical Engineering during my Master’s degree and later completed a PhD in Robotics. During my career, I worked in different areas including robotics, underwater robotics, and control systems, fields in which women were definitely a minority. Today I work in neuromorphic engineering and biomedical AI research, where the environment feels slightly more balanced, although it is still mainly male-dominated.

Making your own way in STEM can be difficult, and at times it has been very hard. I was lucky to grow up in a family that always encouraged me without biases or limitations, and this gave me the confidence to pursue what I liked. However, later in my career I did encounter stereotypes and situations where being a woman in engineering made things more challenging.

Over time, I realized that one of the most important things is to do what you truly enjoy and to surround yourself with people who believe in you and support your growth. Having mentors, colleagues, friends, or family members who encourage you can make a huge difference during difficult moments.

Another important lesson I learned is that making mistakes is completely normal, especially in science and research. Learning often comes through failures, uncertainty, and trial and error. The important thing is not avoiding mistakes completely, but learning how to deal with them, grow from them, and continue moving forward without losing confidence in yourself.

To young women entering STEM today, I would say: stay curious, trust your abilities, and do not let the lack of representation discourage you. Your perspective and contribution are valuable, and science and technology need diverse voices and experiences.

Amelie Reigl

Human Skin Research

Amelie Reigl is a biotech entrepreneur and the Co-founder of TigerShark Science Company. Combining her expertise in developing complex human skin models with active science communication, she challenges traditional stereotypes in STEM.

A message from Amelie

I believe one of the biggest stereotypes I've faced is that many people don't automatically associate femininity with scientific competence. Especially through my participation in Miss Germany and my social media presence, I’ve noticed that some are irritated that someone can wear makeup, enjoy being visible, and at the same time develop complex skin models and run a biotech startup

In the beginning, I often felt I had to prove myself more than others. You sometimes walk into a room and are underestimated before you've even started speaking. But over time, I realized something important: confidence doesn't come from trying to fit into existing expectations, but from trusting your own abilities and pushing forward anyway.

What helped me the most was focusing on competence and purpose rather than trying to please everyone. I learned that visibility is not superficial. Visibility influences what people consider possible. If young women only ever see one specific type of scientist, many will subconsciously feel like they don't belong.

My personal approach to such stereotypes is not to make myself smaller just so others feel more comfortable. I can be scientific, emotional, ambitious, feminine, creative, and entrepreneurial all at once. These are not contradictions.

In my younger years, I struggled with microaggressions. I often felt that my important opinions were not being taken seriously. Furthermore, male researchers were reluctant to invite female researchers to collaborate, leading to feelings of isolation within the community. I was able to overcome these problems by taking initiative and collaborating with many male researchers.

I would tell young women in STEM that they don't have to become 'less of themselves' to be taken seriously. Many think that being confident means never doubting yourself. But honestly, almost everyone in science doubts themselves sometimes. The crucial thing is to keep going regardless.

Furthermore: don't wait until you feel perfectly qualified before seizing opportunities. Apply for that internship. Ask that question. Start that project. Speak up in the meeting. Many careers are built because people had the courage to try something before they felt completely ready.

And another thing: your career doesn't have to follow a traditional path to be successful. Some of the most valuable opportunities in my life came about exactly because I connected different worlds—science, communication, entrepreneurship, creativity, and the public eye. Interdisciplinary paths can feel chaotic at first, but that is exactly where a great strength can lie. And something highly underestimated: build a network!

Christina Sontheim-Leven

Executive Leader & AI Specialist

Christina Sontheim-Leven is a distinguished executive leader, lawyer, and certified AI governance specialist currently serving as an Executive Board Member (CHRO) at CEWE. She leverages her extensive board experience to empower women to claim their space and shape the future of AI.

A message from Christina

Do not wait until you feel 100 percent ready. Many women are still socialized to prove themselves before they claim space, while others claim the space and grow into it. STEM, technology and especially AI urgently need more female perspectives, not later, but now. The future will be shaped by those who understand and use but also design and question this new technology. If women do not take part in that process, their perspectives will be missing in the jobs and decisions of the future.

Furthermore, gender-related challenges in STEM are not only an individual issue. They are structural. Visibility matters because young women need to see that they belong in these fields. Networks matter because careers are not built by performance alone. And courage matters because many systems only change when people are willing to challenge the rules that have silently shaped them for decades.

My advice would therefore be: stay curious, learn the language of technology, build strong networks at an early stage and do not underestimate your own perspective. Especially in AI, diversity of thoughts and perspectives is essential, because biased data and one-sided development teams can reinforce existing inequalities. Women should not only use AI. They should help shape it!

Theoretical Background

The "Leaky Pipeline" Concept

Our project is grounded in academic research exploring why women are underrepresented in STEM fields. Drawing upon the foundational research by Jacob Clark Blickenstaff (2005) on gender filters in science careers, we aim to understand and address the structural challenges that filter out female talent at various stages of their careers.
